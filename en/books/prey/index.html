Prey

by Michael Crichton

My note

Prey feels like a cautionary tale about the limits of control—whether over technology, markets, or even ourselves. It reminds me that true sovereignty starts with honest self-awareness, something that’s harder than it seems but essential if we want to navigate long-term shifts without getting blindsided. Thought you’d appreciate how it blends that uneasy future tech with questions about human nature and responsibility.

It must be something like that, because we know that people can think about themselves indefinitely. Some people think of little else. Yet people never seem to change as a result of their intensive introspection. They never understand themselves better. It’s very rare to find genuine self-knowledge.

I always figured something similar must happen when people turn their psychological insight-apparatus on themselves. The brain hangs. The thought process goes and goes, but it doesn’t get anywhere.

The same people who are coldly clear-eyed about the world around them have nothing but fantasies about themselves. Psychological knowledge doesn’t work if you look in a mirror. This bizarre fact is, as far as I know, unexplained.

There’s one problem with all psychological knowledge—nobody can apply it to themselves. People can be incredibly astute about the shortcomings of their friends, spouses, children. But they have no insight into themselves at all.

As long as she wasn’t shooting up, or breaking their bones, she was a fit mother in the eyes of the court.
