Naked Statistics

by Charles Wheelan

My note

Numbers tell stories, but they don’t always tell the truth—this book reminded me how easy it is to be fooled by impressive stats without asking the right questions. In a world where clarity and judgment are rare, especially around money and power, understanding the subtle dance between correlation and causation feels like a quiet act of financial sovereignty. Thought you’d appreciate how it nudges me to think bigger while acting smaller, even in the way I see data.

Correlation measures the degree to which two phenomena are related to one another.

The overall lesson of this chapter is that statistical malfeasance has very little to do with bad math. If anything, impressive calculations can obscure nefarious motives. The fact that you’ve calculated the mean correctly will not alter the fact that the median is a more accurate indicator.

Of course, the median can also do its share of dissembling because it is not sensitive to outliers.

Statistical arguments have much in common with bad marriages; the disputants often talk past one another.

Even the most precise and accurate descriptive statistics can suffer from a more fundamental problem: a lack of clarity over what exactly we are trying to define, describe, or explain.

Once there are multiple ways of describing the same thing (e.g., “he’s got a great personality” or “he was convicted of securities fraud”), the descriptive statistics that we choose to use (or not to use) will have a profound impact on the impression that we leave.

Descriptive statistics are often used to compare two figures or quantities.

The mean is the middle line which is often represented by the Greek letter µ. The standard deviation is often represented by the Greek letter σ. Each band represents one standard deviation.

The beauty of the normal distribution—its Michael Jordan power, finesse, and elegance—comes from the fact that we know by definition exactly what proportion of the observations in a normal distribution lie within one standard deviation of the mean (68.2 percent), within two standard deviations of the mean (95.4 percent), within three standard deviations (99.7 percent), and so on.

Data that are distributed normally are symmetrical around their mean in a bell shape that will look familiar to you.

This is a good time to introduce one of the most important, helpful, and common distributions in statistics: the normal distribution.

For many typical distributions of data, a high proportion of the observations lie within one standard deviation of the mean (meaning that they are in the range from one standard deviation below the mean to one standard deviation above the mean).

The standard deviation is the descriptive statistic that allows us to assign a single number to this dispersion around the mean.

The weights of the airline passengers are “more spread out,” which is an important attribute when it comes to describing the weights of these two groups.

On the basis of the descriptive tools introduced so far, the weights of the airline passengers and the marathoners are nearly identical. But they’re not. Yes, the weights of the two groups have roughly the same “middle,” but the airline passengers have far more dispersion around that midpoint, meaning that their weights are spread farther from the midpoint.

In other words, how spread out are the observations?

Another statistic that can help us describe what might otherwise be a jumble of numbers is the standard deviation, which is a measure of how dispersed the data are from their mean.

The benefit of these kinds of descriptive statistics is that they describe where a particular observation lies compared with everyone else.

We can go even further and divide the distribution into hundredths, or percentiles.

Or the distribution can be divided into deciles, each with 10 percent of the observations.

The first quartile consists of the bottom 25 percent of the observations; the second quartile consists of the next 25 percent of the observations; and so on.

As we’ve already discussed, the median divides a distribution in half. The distribution can be further divided into quarters, or quartiles.

Neither the median nor the mean is hard to calculate; the key is determining which measure of the “middle” is more accurate in a particular situation (a phenomenon that is easily exploited).

So, to return to the title chapter, what is the point of learning statistics? To summarize huge quantities of data. To make better decisions. To answer important social questions. To recognize patterns that can refine how we do everything from selling diapers to catching criminals.

This book will walk through many of the most common statistical errors and misrepresentations (so that you can recognize them, not put them to use).

the mathematical precision attached to statistical analysis can dress up some serious nonsense.

The reality is that you can lie with statistics.

But who says that everyone using statistics is smart or honest? As mentioned, this book began as an homage to How to Lie with Statistics, which was first published in 1954 and has sold over a million copies.

Statistical analysis is more like good detective work (hence the commercial potential of CSI: Regression Analysis). Smart and honest people will often disagree about what the data are trying to tell us.

“statistically significant” finding, which means that the analysis has uncovered an association between two variables that is not likely to be the product of chance alone.

Regression analysis is the tool that enables researchers to isolate a relationship between two variables, such as smoking and cancer, while holding constant (or “controlling for”) the effects of other important variables, such as diet, exercise, weight, and so on.

Statistical analysis is the detective work that crafts the raw data into some meaningful conclusion.

Statistics does basically the same thing. The data present unorganized clues—the crime scene.

any model to deal with risk must have probability as its foundation.

One important statistical practice is sampling, which is the process of gathering data for a small area, say, a handful of census tracts, and then using those data to make an informed judgment, or inference, about the homeless population for the city as a whole.

we can use data from the “known world” to make informed inferences about the “unknown world.”

Descriptive statistics exist to simplify, which always implies some loss of nuance or detail.

But one theme of this book will be that an overreliance on any descriptive statistic can lead to misleading conclusions, or cause undesirable behavior.

Statistics rarely offers a single “right” way of doing anything.

Swedish mathematician and writer Andrejs Dunkels: It’s easy to lie with statistics, but it’s hard to tell the truth without them.

If you want to understand the numbers behind the news and to appreciate the extraordinary (and growing) power of data, this is the stuff you need to know.

The book has been designed to introduce the statistical concepts with the most relevance to everyday life.

This book will not make you a statistical expert; it will teach you enough care and respect for the field that you don’t do the statistical equivalent of blowing someone’s head off.

Statistics is like a high-caliber weapon: helpful when used correctly and potentially disastrous in the wrong hands.
