From Here to Financial Happiness

by Jonathan Clements

My note

Thinking about every financial choice as a tradeoff really resonates with me—whether it’s holding Bitcoin or deciding how we spend our time and money. Jonathan’s take on valuing experiences over possessions and planning with humility feels like a quiet reminder to align our actions with long-term freedom, not just short-term satisfaction. It’s a thoughtful nudge to keep our eyes on the bigger picture, while appreciating the journey along the way.

My highlights

10 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

Spend on experiences or possessions? Experiences deliver fond memories. Possessions deliver repair bills.

others, whether by getting ahead at work or by flaunting status

We got a lot of satisfaction from working toward the promotion and we were excited as we anticipated the house purchase. The destination may have proven less thrilling than we had hoped, but the journey was great fun.

Whenever we open our wallets, we should think not only about what we are getting but also what we're giving up.

There is, as economists like to say, an opportunity cost – and yet we often fail to ponder the opportunities forgone.

If we purchase one item, we can't buy something else. If we spend money today, we can't save it for tomorrow. If we decide we want the big house, we'll have less for other goals, like the kids' college and our own retirement.

Everything's a Tradeoff

Things that make us feel good today – spending, eating junk food, boozing it up – often leave us feeling worse tomorrow.

Life shouldn't be an impulse purchase. We may fall short of our financial plans, but that's better than having no plan at all.
