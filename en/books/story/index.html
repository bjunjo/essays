Story

by Robert McKee

My note

Originality feels like the foundation of everything I value—whether allocating capital or crafting my own narrative in this shifting world. McKee’s take on story reminds me that meaningful connection requires both clarity of vision and respect for the audience’s intelligence, a balance I try to keep in mind as I think long term and embrace financial sovereignty. It’s a reminder that shaping a story, like building a portfolio, is about thoughtful choices that reflect who you are and who you want to reach.

My highlights

25 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

Originality is the confluence of content and form—distinctive choices of subject plus a unique shaping of the telling.

Story is about originality, not duplication.

For without it, the creative act is pointless.

The audience is a force as determining of story design as any other element.

You must shape your story in a way that both expresses your vision and satisfies the audience’s desires.

The audience is not only smart, it’s smarter than most films, and that fact won’t change when you move to the other side of the screen.

When talented people write well, it is generally for this reason: They’re moved by a desire to touch the audience.

When talented people write badly it’s generally for one of two reasons: Either they’re blinded by an idea they feel compelled to prove or they’re driven by an emotion they must express.

Story is about respect, not disdain, for the audience.

Hollywood’s best acting and directing talents are acutely aware that their careers depend on working within quality writing.

Story is about the realities, not the mysteries of writing.

Story is about thoroughness, not shortcuts.

We do not wish to escape life but to find life, to use our minds in fresh, experimental ways, to flex our emotions, to enjoy, to learn, to add depth to our days.

We go to the movies to enter a new, fascinating world, to inhabit vicariously another human being who at first seems so unlike us and yet at heart is like us, to live in a fictional reality that illuminates our daily reality.

once inside this alien world, we find ourselves. Deep within these characters and their conflicts we discover our own humanity.

Stereotypical stories stay at home, archetypal stories travel.

It confines itself to a narrow, culture-specific experience and dresses in stale, nonspecific generalities.

A stereotypical story reverses this pattern: It suffers a poverty of both content and form.

The archetypal story unearths a universally human experience, then wraps itself inside a unique, culture-specific expression.

Story is about archetypes, not stereotypes.

Story urges the creation of works that will excite audiences on the six continents and live in revival for decades. No one needs yet another recipe book on how to reheat Hollywood leftovers.

Story is about eternal, universal forms, not formulas.

Anxious, inexperienced writers obey rules. Rebellious, unschooled writers break rules. Artists master the form.

A rule says, “You must do it this way.” A principle says, “This works … and has through all remembered time.”

Story is about principles, not rules.
