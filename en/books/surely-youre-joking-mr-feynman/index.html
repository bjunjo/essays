"Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman!"

by Richard P. Feynman, Ralph Leighton, Edward Hutchings, and Albert R. Hibbs

My note

Feynman’s curiosity and relentless puzzle drive remind me that deep understanding often means questioning the standard symbols and frameworks we take for granted—something I try to do when allocating capital or thinking about Bitcoin’s long game. His playful yet rigorous approach is a good nudge to stay humble and keep probing beneath the surface, especially in a world that rewards short-term noise over thoughtful exploration. Hope you find his blend of mischief and insight as refreshing as I do.

My highlights

10 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

I learned there that innovation is a very difficult thing in the real world.

The world is full of this kind of dumb smart-alec who doesn’t understand anything.

I realized then that if I’m going to talk to anybody else, I’ll have to use the standard symbols, so I eventually gave up my own symbols.

Once when I was explaining something to another kid in high school, without thinking I started to make these symbols, and he said, “What the hell are those?”

I thought my symbols were just as good, if not better, than the regular symbols—it doesn’t make any difference what symbols you use—but I discovered later that it does make a difference.

Other times, my way was most clever—the standard demonstration in the book was much more complicated!

Sometimes, for a thing where I didn’t notice a simple way to do it, I went all over the place till I got it.

Another thing I did in high school was to invent problems and theorems. I mean, if I were doing any mathematical thing at all, I would find some practical example for which it would be useful.

That’s a puzzle drive. It’s what accounts for my wanting to decipher Mayan hieroglyphics, for trying to open safes.

I can’t just leave it after I’ve found out so much about it. I have to keep going to find out ultimately what is the matter with it in the end.
