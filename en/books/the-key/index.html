The Key

by James N. Frey

My note

The way Frey unpacks the power of the word as a force that shapes myths and reality itself really hits home for me—especially in a world where narratives can either enslave or liberate. It reminds me that true sovereignty starts with mastering the stories we tell ourselves and others, a kind of capital allocation for the mind and soul. Holding that awareness feels like the first step toward long-term freedom, both financial and personal.

And the Word … was God. Indeed it was. And still is.

The ancient peoples of the world knew the power of the word. In the Old Testament, the Hebrew Scriptures, God created the heavens and the earth not by waving a magic wand, but by speaking words.

You, as a fiction writer, have the pen in your hand. What you create may have an enormous impact on individuals, communities, nations, the world—and world history.

See Leni Riefenstahl’s Triumph of the Will (1935), and you can see the effect of the Nazi myth on its followers. Myth indeed is a potent force.

Over the next few decades, hundreds of young men were found dead with a pistol in one hand, a love note in the other, and a copy of Young Werther in their back pockets.

Because of the power of men to create myth, Percy Shelley, the nineteenth-century poet, called poets and fiction writers “the unacknowledged legislators of the world.”

When a myth is believed as true, it’s a powerful force. People have been killing each other over myths and their interpretation since, well, who knows?

We in the West, too, have our mythologies. The Free Man, as an example. Think you’re a “free” American? Tell it to the IRS.

Millions of people are being imprisoned and put to death in the name of the communist myth in Cuba, Serbia, China, and Tibet. And many more will die in its name before the myth is dead and buried.

Action-adventure films are almost always hero’s journeys and are often huge hits. There’s the Star Wars saga, of course, which was written with Joseph Campbell’s The Hero with a Thousand Faces in mind.

The outer struggle is against fabulous forces in the Mythological Woods, where a victory may be won; the inner struggle is to grow through self-discovery and achieve a transformation of character.

The mythological hero, modern or ancient, is on a journey that involves an outer and an inner struggle.

“A hero ventures forth from the world of common day into a region of supernatural wonder: fabulous forces are there encountered and a decisive victory is won: the hero comes back from this mysterious adventure with the power to bestow boons on his fellow man.”

According to Campbell, the monomyth is structurally a reenactment of the same mythological hero’s journey; it is prevalent in all cultures, in every era, from the dim beginnings of human consciousness eons ago to the present.
