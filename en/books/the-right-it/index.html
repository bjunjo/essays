The Right It

by Alberto Savoia

My note

Finding The Right It feels a lot like spotting true value in a sea of noise—something I think about constantly when allocating capital or navigating Bitcoin’s shifting landscape. Alberto’s reminder that real validation happens outside Thoughtland, through messy, real-world experiments, resonates deeply with how I approach long-term bets and sovereignty: it’s less about perfect ideas and more about putting the right ones to the test. Hope it sparks some of that same restless curiosity.

The first line in the Law of Market Failure states: “Most new products will fail in the market.” By using the word most, I am implying that more than 50% of all new products fail.

It’s important to be explicit about your success criteria before you get started.

A movie that garners critical praise but flops at the box office is still a market failure—at least for those who invested in it hoping to make a profit.

It’s important to note that some products can be a market failure but still be considered successful based on other criteria.

Market success means any actual market result from an investment in a new product that matches or surpasses the expected result.

If you launch your product into the market and the actual results are less than or the opposite of what you expected, that’s what we call a market failure.

Consider this your first hard fact, and remember that hard facts are your friends even if they may not appear so at first glance.

Failure is the most likely outcome.

This is true in art, in science, in relationships—everywhere. And it’s most definitely true in the world of business. Most new businesses fail, and most new products launched by existing businesses fail.

When it comes to bringing new product ideas to market, failure is always an option. In fact, it is—by far—the most likely outcome anytime anyone tries to do something new or different.
