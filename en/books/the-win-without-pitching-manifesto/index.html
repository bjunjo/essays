The Win Without Pitching Manifesto

by Blair Enns

My note

Being clear about what you truly specialize in feels like the ultimate act of financial sovereignty—knowing where to place your bets and when to say no. Enns’ manifesto reminds me that focus isn’t just a business strategy; it’s a mindset that shapes how we allocate capital, time, and energy for the long haul. It’s a quiet rebellion against chaos, and that’s a rhythm I’m learning to embrace every day.

My highlights

21 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

The revolution we must fight is within. There is no enemy. We are victims only of a creative mind that makes choosing a focus more difficult for us than most.

The problem has always been us, and our struggle with focus. We are at the root of our free-pitching problem and we alone have the power to free ourselves from the pitch.

The good news is that there is no fun like making money, because financial strength affords us all kinds of options in our business and personal lives.

Our personal desire for variety is suddenly placed at odds with the fundamental need of our business to focus.

This is our desire as artists – to satisfy our curiosity and solve the problems we haven’t previously solved.

business development can be viewed as the polite battle for control.

For us to do our best work we need to leverage our outside perspective. We need to be allowed to lead the engagement. We need to take control.

We are hired for our expertise and not our service. It is a mistake to believe that the service sector mantra of “The customer is always right” applies to us. Like any engagement of expertise, we often enter into ours with the client not truly knowing what he needs, let alone recognizing the route to a solution.

for price elasticity is tied to the availability of substitutes.

A Price Premium → To command a price premium means that when we win, we do so not by cutting price, but while charging more.

A Sales Advantage → To possess a sales advantage means that when and where we choose to compete, we win more often than not.

Too often, we decide to not decide and so, in our minds, leave open the possibility that we may continue to do all things for all types of clients.

The first step – focus – is to answer the strategy question of “What business are we in?” Choosing the focus for our firm remains The Difficult Business Decision.

We must choose a focus Then articulate that focus via a consistent claim of expertise And finally, we must work to add the missing skills, capabilities and processes necessary to support our new claim.

Positioning is the foundation of business development success, and of business success.

When the alternatives to hiring us are many, the client will dictate price.

Power in the client-agency relationship usually rests with the client. His power comes from the alternatives that he sees to hiring us.

It is expertise and expertise alone that will set us apart in a meaningful way and allow us to deal with our clients and prospects from a position of power.

Expertise is the only valid basis for differentiating ourselves from the competition. Not personality. Not process. Not price.

The world is drowning in undifferentiated creative businesses.

If we are not seen as more expert than our competition then we will be viewed as one in a sea of many, and we will have little power in our relationships with our clients and prospects.
