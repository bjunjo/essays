Breath

by James Nestor

My note

Breath reminded me how something as simple as the way we inhale shapes not just our bodies but our long-term well-being—a quiet form of sovereignty over the self. It’s a powerful reminder that small, mindful adjustments today can compound into healthier tomorrows, much like thoughtful capital allocation. Hope it nudges you to breathe deeper and think bigger, Elsa.

“Be careful not to have the breath inhaled through the mouth.”

Mouthbreathing causes the body to lose 40 percent more water.

“For they that sleep with their Jaws extended, by reason of their breath, and the air tossed to and fro, have their tongues and palates dry, and desire to be moistened by drinking in the night.”

“More wholesome to sleep . . . with the mouth shut,” wrote Levinus Lemnius, a Dutch physician from the 1500s who was credited as one of the first researchers to study snoring.

Inhaling from the nose has the opposite effect. It forces air against all those flabby tissues at the back of the throat, making the airways wider and breathing easier.

Mouthbreathing, it turns out, changes the physical body and transforms airways, all for the worse.

Finding the best heart rate for exercise is easy: subtract your age from 180. The result is the maximum your body can withstand to stay in the aerobic state.

Evolution doesn’t always mean progress, Evans told me. It means change. And life can change for better or worse.

I call this a “lost art” because so many of these new discoveries aren’t new at all.

It explains how the billions and billions of molecules you bring in with each breath have built your bones, sheaths of muscle, blood, brains, and organs, and the emerging science of how these microscopic bits will influence your health and happiness tomorrow, next week, next month, next year, and decades from now.

This book is a scientific adventure into the lost art and science of breathing. It explores the transformation that occurs inside our bodies every 3.3 seconds, the time it takes the average person to inhale and exhale.

On a more inspiring note, some of these researchers were also showing that many modern maladies—asthma, anxiety, attention deficit hyperactivity disorder, psoriasis, and more—could either be reduced or reversed simply by changing the way we inhale and exhale.

this breathing research has been taking place elsewhere: in the muddy digs of ancient burial sites, the easy chairs of dental offices, and the rubber rooms of mental hospitals. Not the kinds of places where you’d expect to find cutting-edge research into a biological function.

The point is to get air in and let the body do the rest.

Breathing, for all these people, for all these cultures, was powerful medicine.
