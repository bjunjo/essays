Poor Charlie’s Almanack

by Charles T. Munger, Peter D. Kaufman, John Collison, and Warren Buffett

My note

Charlie’s disciplined approach to knowing what not to do and patiently waiting for the right opportunity feels like a blueprint for capital allocation—and honestly, for life in general. His focus on intellectual humility and lifelong learning resonates deeply with how I think about building financial sovereignty and staying true to my circles of competence. Hope you find his blend of wisdom and practicality as grounding as I do.

If Ben suggested saving pennies, Charlie raised the stakes. If Ben said be prompt, Charlie said be early.

Initially a mere disciple of Ben’s, Charlie was soon breaking new ground. What Ben had recommended, Charlie demanded.

From 1733 to 1758, Ben Franklin dispensed useful and timeless advice through Poor Richard’s Almanack. Among the virtues extolled were thrift, duty, hard work, and simplicity.

Although Charlie didn’t invent the concept of compounding growth, his success, and that of Berkshire Hathaway, is a testament to its existence.

His essays extol the virtues of free enterprise, yes, but also of doing business the right way, with integrity and rigor. Of taking your work very seriously, but never yourself.

It’s not just a book about investing; it’s a guide to learning how to think for yourself to understand the world around you.

Poor Charlie’s Almanack is a testament to the power of thinking across disciplines.
