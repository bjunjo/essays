Career Advice for Uniquely Ambitious People

by Eric Jorgenson

My note

Finding ways to create real value and capture it for yourself feels like the core of any worthwhile pursuit—something this book lays out with a clear-eyed honesty I respect. It’s a reminder that autonomy and long-term thinking aren’t just ideals but practical tools for building a life where you can truly own your time and impact, much like how I approach capital allocation and bitcoin. Thought you might appreciate how it cuts through the noise around “career” and gets to what really matters.

Your job is respected and admired to the degree that you contribute to society.

Seek accountability and responsibility and succeed, you’ll be rewarded and celebrated. Carefully avoid responsibility and accountability and you will be neither rewarded nor cared about.

How Bad Do The Worst Do?

So, when you are choosing a career, look up. Look at the ceiling. See how high you can go, and if you plan to work your ass off -- do it somewhere with room to rise.

most jobs have an entrepreneurial option, which can create a ton of leverage and create some more extreme outliers.

Lawyers, Doctors, Chefs, Plumbers, Contractors… those are probably capped near a 10:1 (which is great compared to a typical 2:1 career.)

Even some important and skilled careers are under this band: Teaching, Accounting… most corporate jobs fall into this, because the feedback loops are just too unclear.

This does not imply that everyone should go to these roles with extreme outliers. However, most ambitious people will want to avoid careers where the maximum ratio is smaller than 2:1.

The best CEOs, software developers, or salespeople might make 100 or 1,000 times more than average. These careers have a very wide distribution of skill. The best can be much, much, much better than the worst.

Everyone wants to be good at their jobs. And if you’re reading this, it is likely that you will outperform the average. Yay for you. The next important question is: “How much does that matter?”

Being good at your job should mean helping people -- or at the very least not hurting them.

Find a career where your incentives are aligned with stakeholders so you’re not miserable and hated… like everyone in congress.

Fast, clear feedback loops become much more effective when they deliver a prize or a sting every time they provide you with an answer.

Set high goals and short deadlines. Find jobs with compensation structures that are based on your success and your progress.

The goal is to get far away from apathy, so that you are doing your very very best on every attempt. You never have an important piece of your job where you can “check out” and not care about the outcome.

You need to have a reward for victories and feel pain for failures.

If you want to learn fast and become smart, respected, and successful quickly you have to have something at stake.

You never run faster than when there’s a lion on your heels, and you never fight harder than when there’s no other way out.

Examples of Jobs with Clear, Fast Feedback Loops: -          Realtor -          Short-cycle Sales -          Programming -          Trades (electrical, plumbing)

There are lot of obstacles to overcome. If you want to be judged on objective criteria rather than subjective -- find clear, fast feedback loops.

The more ways someone can pick apart your contributions and call you “lucky”, the more ways they can find to underpay you or pass you by. When you do work that provides immediate value that is obviously the outcome of your effort, you will be empowered to earn what you have created.

If you are committed to excellence, and you want to remove any doubt about your excellence, find a clear, fast feedback loop.

And it is the same with feedback loops in your career and learning -- the more closely tied effort and reward are, the faster you will learn and the longer the lessons will last.

We watch TV instead of read because it provides immediate rewards. We don’t save money because there is no immediate reward… work and reward are too far apart.

The most likely ways to destroy your life are cases of near-term pleasure, long-term pain. The failure to respect the long-term dangers of short-term rewards is incredibly dangerous.

Humans are very good at learning with fast feedback loops. When something feels good or bad immediately, we learn the lesson.

There is no confusion about whether a great door-to-door salesman is great, or a bad programmer is bad.

In an environment like Venture Capital, with long cycle times and very high unpredictability, there are almost certainly some very bad investors who look very good, and some very good investors who look very bad.

Computers provide very fast feedback loops. Cooking provides fast feedback loops. Even within the same industry, context can change your learning speed: Door-to-door sales provides very fast feedback loops. Long-cycle enterprise sales has much slower feedback loops.

Faster feedback loops also allow for much quicker iteration. The ability to see an effect immediately and adjust your very next cause in order to see the results of that experiment are incredibly effective.

Faster feedback loops are inherently more clear.

Faster = More Clear Timing and clarity are heavily interrelated, because the faster a feedback loop happens, the less time there is for interference from the environment.

If a feedback loop is immediate, lessons are learned faster and more clearly, and have a lasting impact.

In unclear-but-not-insanely-unpredictable feedback loops (like sales), competition will help you measure your progress.

Clearly determining cause and effect in emotional, psychological, opaque individual humans is difficult… let alone the complexity of a whole ecosystem of them. Outcomes are unpredictable, and it takes more experiments and more effort in research (as well as extreme discipline) to use this as an instructive feedback loop.

The harder it is to learn what “works” and gain confidence in your skillset, to be able to demonstrate your value.

Brand advertising has an unclear feedback loop, due to low predictability.

Some jobs (like consulting projects) have no feedback loops at all, if the product of your work never is tested against reality in a way that you can observe and learn from.

If cause and effect are closely tied, you can learn lessons definitively.

Timing: How quickly do cause and effect relate?

Clarity: How direct cause and effect are correlated.

Feedback loops determine how fast you learn and improve.

Overlapping skills will set you up to win more opportunities, and be more uniquely valuable to places where you can really apply all of your talents and create much more value than an average hire could.

Another way to create specialization is by mastering two (or more) skills, instead of one. Overlapping perspectives that will give you a unique advantage in your field, or with a certain kind of client.

You can specialize by being a unique generalist.

Then you can sit there with people with sexy jobs, quietly smiling at the fact that you are tripling their income and they have no idea. That’s the life.

Seriously. Just ask for it. Not right away, not the first meeting… but once you have established rapport and connection, and you know and trust them -- Ask. For. A. Job.

These problems are your answer key. When (if) you eventually ask to work with them, you should speak toward the problems you have learned about, and how you can help solve them.

As you’re getting to know a mentor or possible manager, you should be constantly looking for ways to help them.

The best way to get a powerful person’s attention: offer to help them.

You are far more likely to move toward your goals by helping other people with theirs than by waiting for them to offer to help you with yours.

Go to coffees, get on calls, and send good follow-up emails. Ask smart questions, do your homework, and always be on time. Read continuously, send authors notes, and remember that no one is immune to a kind word.

You’ve got to navigate the maze of human relationships to find your soulmate manager. That means being proactive, and creating a habit of continuing to make progress by meeting people and getting to know them.

Get that at all costs, then work in other attributes around it.

The point is that a lovely manager is the first box to check.

This isn’t to say avoid known companies, sexy industries, or attractive roles… just that they aren’t the main priority.

You’re sacrificing short-term social status (with strangers) for quality of life, a stronger set of skills, and long-term value creation. Understand this trade-off, and embrace it to reduce the temptation into the more-crowded, less-thoughtful path.

The long game has costs. And there are always temptations from the path.
