Books

14 books that actually changed how I think — about money, Bitcoin, and life. With my Kindle highlights.

Personal Finance

Rich Dad Poor Dad Robert T. Kiyosaki · 57 highlights A classic. The asset vs. liability distinction — what puts money in your pocket versus what takes it out — is one of the most useful mental models I've picked up. Simple, almost obvious once you hear it. But most people never do.
I Will Teach You to Be Rich Ramit Sethi · 348 highlights Read this in college. Stayed up all night — time just disappeared. It was the first book that made personal finance feel actually doable for a beginner. Not intimidating, not preachy. Just practical.
The Simple Path to Wealth JL Collins · 104 highlights The clearest case for index investing I've read. Collins strips away all the noise and gets to the point. One of the books that helped me understand how to actually build wealth without overcomplicating it.

Bitcoin

The Internet of Money Andreas M. Antonopoulos · 48 highlights I came to this through YouTube, then the paper book, then back again. Antonopoulos explains Bitcoin not as a price chart but as a protocol — why it matters at a deeper level. This is where it really clicked for me.
Inventing Bitcoin Yan Pritzker · 45 highlights A great book by Yan Pritzker. It explains how Bitcoin actually works — the mechanics, the design decisions, why it's built the way it is. Short and precise. If you want to understand Bitcoin from the inside, start here.
What Is Money? The Saylor Series Michael Saylor & Robert Breedlove · 278 highlights This is where I developed a deeper understanding of Bitcoin. Saylor's framework for money as energy storage across time changed how I think about capital allocation entirely.

Wealth & Systems

The Almanack of Naval Ravikant Eric Jorgenson · 448 highlights More than any other book, this one shaped how I think about wealth, happiness, and freedom. Naval's ideas on specific knowledge, leverage, and judgment have compounded in my thinking ever since.
How to Fail at Almost Everything and Still Win Big Scott Adams · 302 highlights Systems over goals. That reframe alone is worth the whole book. Adams is one of the clearest thinkers I've found on how to structure your life for long-term optionality — and how to think probabilistically about success.
The 4-Hour Workweek Tim Ferriss · 218 highlights I read this in the Korean military. It rewired how I think about time and work. The big lesson: effectiveness over efficiency. It's not about how well you do something — it's about whether you should be doing it at all. That distinction stuck with me.

Investing

Fooled by Randomness Nassim Nicholas Taleb · 160 highlights I read all of Taleb's books, mostly in paper. This one hit hardest. The warning that success is often skill wearing the costume of luck is one I keep coming back to. A necessary corrective for anyone who thinks they're good at investing.
What I Learned Losing a Million Dollars Jim Paul & Brendan Moynihan · 107 highlights More useful than most books on how to win — because it's honest about how you lose. The psychology of loss is the part most investors never study. This book taught me what to be careful about, which ended up being more valuable than tips on what to do.

Philosophy

Meditations Marcus Aurelius · 193 highlights A Roman emperor writing to himself about how to be better. What got me was the rawness of it — even at the top of the world, the same human struggles. He was genuinely wrestling with the fundamentals of how to live. The Stoic frame has been quietly useful ever since.
Letters from a Stoic Lucius Annaeus Seneca · 105 highlights Seneca was one of the wealthiest men in Rome and still wrote obsessively about not being owned by wealth. Reading his letters to a friend — the back-and-forth on how to actually live — is a good way to understand Stoicism beyond the quotes.

Life

The Defining Decade Meg Jay · 395 highlights Read this in my early 20s. It gave me a framework for why the decisions you make in your 20s compound harder than any other decade. It became a foundation for how I thought about that period of my life. I still recommend it to everyone under 30.
