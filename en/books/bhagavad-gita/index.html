Bhagavad Gita

by Stephen Mitchell

My note

There’s a quiet power in embracing action without attachment to results—something I’m still learning as I navigate capital and life. The Bhagavad Gita reminds me that true sovereignty starts when you focus on your duty with clarity and let go of the noise around outcomes. Thinking ₿ig means holding steady through uncertainty, and this book captures that mindset in a way only timeless wisdom can.

11 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

With no desire for success, no anxiety about failure, indifferent to results, he burns up his actions in the fire of wisdom.

It is better to do your own duty badly, than to perfectly do another’s; you are safe from harm when you do what you should be doing.

Though the unwise cling to their actions, watching for results, the wise are free of attachments, and act for the well-being of the whole world.

Abandoning all desires, acting without craving, free from all thoughts of “I” and “mine,” that man finds utter peace.

When a man gives up all desires that emerge from the mind, and rests contented in the Self by the Self, he is called a man of firm wisdom.

The wise man lets go of all results, whether good or bad, and is focused on the action alone. Yoga is skill in actions.

Self-possessed, resolute, act without any thought of results, open to success or failure. This equanimity is yoga.

You have a right to your actions, but never to your actions’ fruits. Act for the action’s sake. And do not be attached to inaction.

Indifferent to gain or loss, to victory or defeat, prepare yourself for the battle and do not succumb to sin.

Everything essential that it points to—what we call wisdom or radiance or peace—is already present inside us.

The healthiest way to begin reading and absorbing a text like the Bhagavad Gita is to understand that ultimately it has nothing to teach.
