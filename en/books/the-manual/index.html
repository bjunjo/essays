The Manual

by Epictetus, Ancient Renewal, Sam Torode

My note

Wrestling with what’s truly mine—my time, my choices, my mindset—feels more urgent than ever in a world obsessed with external validation. This book’s reminder to focus on what’s within my power echoes the core of financial sovereignty and long-term capital allocation: control your lens, and everything else aligns. It’s a steadying compass for navigating life and markets without losing sight of what really belongs to me.

My highlights

20 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

The only thing that can impede your will is your will itself.

Do not wish that all things will go well with you, but that you will go well with all things.

Likewise in life, remain steadfast in pursuing your mission, always willing to shed distractions.

What, then, is your own? The way you live your life.

Do not take satisfaction in possessions and achievements that are not your own.

People who are ignorant of philosophy blame others for their own misfortunes. Those who are beginning to learn philosophy blame themselves. Those who have mastered philosophy blame no one.

Your condition is the result of your own opinions and interpretations.

When you are feeling upset, angry, or sad, don’t blame another for your state of mind.

People are not disturbed by things themselves, but by the views they take of those things.

When you embrace your wife or child, remember that they are mortal beings. By accepting their nature rather than denying it, if either should die you will find the strength to bear it.

If you have a favorite cup, remember that it is only a cup that you prefer—if it is broken, you can bear it.

For if you desire things not within your power, you will suffer disappointment.

End the habit of despising things that are not within your power, and apply your aversion to things that are within your power.

Then ask whether it falls within or outside your sphere of power. And, if it is beyond your power to control, let it go.

Whenever distress or displeasure arises in your mind, remind yourself, “This is only my interpretation, not reality itself.”

If you want not just peace and contentment, but power and wealth too, you may forfeit the former in seeking the latter, and will lose your freedom and happiness along the way.

If you wish to have peace and contentment, release your attachment to all things outside your control. This is the path of freedom and happiness.

But if you focus your attention only on what is truly your own concern, and leave to others what concerns them, then you will be in charge of your interior life.

Working within our sphere of control, we are naturally free, independent, and strong. Beyond that sphere, we are weak, limited, and dependent.

There are things that are within our power, and things that fall outside our power. Within our power are our own opinions, aims, desires, dislikes—in sum, our own thoughts and actions. Outside our power are our physical characteristics, the class into which we were born, our reputation in the eyes of others, and honors and offices that may be bestowed on us.
