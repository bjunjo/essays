How to Leave Bitcoin to Your Heirs

by Anthony S. Park

My note

Planning how to pass on Bitcoin isn’t just about the tech—it’s about protecting the freedom and sovereignty we value so much. Park’s approach to balancing simplicity with security feels like the kind of thoughtful capital allocation I respect: setting up for the long game without overcomplicating today. This is exactly the kind of clarity we need when thinking about what legacy really means.

On top of these potential changes in our life circumstances, we have to consider that bitcoin is still relatively new and evolving.

You may be thinking: I’m not worried about those things because I would update my inheritance plan if any of them happened. Unlikely. Of those surveyed who even have a plan, 70% say their existing plan is not up to date.

So don’t think of your plan as a zero-sum decision. Rather, think of it as your baselayer of fail-safes.

Why? Because if you think of it with too much finality (“This is how my heirs will inherit when I die, so it’d better be perfect!”), making the decision will feel too big, triggering analysis paralysis.

You may find it helpful to mentally think of this bitcoin estate plan as just a baselayer, rather than something set in stone.

And don’t stress about it too much, because the bitcoin community will in all likelihood develop even better solutions between now and when you die.

Don’t let the perfect be the enemy of the good. Accept that (for now) you need a plan that will have trade-offs.
