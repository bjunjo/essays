For Everything a Season

by Joan Chittister

My note

Loss isn’t just an end—it’s a reset button for how we live and think, something I’ve come to appreciate both in capital allocation and life. This book’s reminder that true freedom comes from embracing new beginnings resonates deeply with how I approach risk and sovereignty, especially when the small choices today shape the legacy we leave tomorrow. Hope you find its quiet courage as grounding as I do.

Loss is simply another entrance into life.

What is true is that losing something can, in fact, be the beginning of an exciting new world, a totally new life, a completely different and even more satisfying way of being.

What is true is that we ourselves are more than however much we strive to achieve. What is false is that losing anything will be the end of us.

Loss can be a lovely and freeing thing. It gives to a few what is too often missing to the many. It provides a chance for a person to start over in life, to scrape away the barnacles of time and empty out the accretions of the years.

“What God gave Adam,” Elie Wiesel wrote, “was not forgiveness from sin. What God gave Adam was the right to begin again.” If we want to be mentally healthy, if we want to live full and vibrant lives, we need to remember the lesson of the Garden of Eden. It is the fine art of learning to be wrong.

For those who know loss, life cries out for fulfillment, and possibility is forever new.

The loss of the job becomes the beginning of the new and better career. The end of the money means the opportunity to extricate ourselves from the counterfeit lifestyle that had sedated our minds and plasticized our souls. Loss is surely meant to be an invitation to options.

That fact is that loss is not only, not always, bad, but also is sometimes great good in disguise.

This time, indeed, is my only time to be born. On it, in fact, depend the two pillars of my life: my freedom of soul and my eternal self-esteem.

What happens here now is my responsibility. What happens tomorrow is my legacy to it. It is not a matter of doing great things. No, it is far worse than that. It is a matter of doing small things courageously.

Personal development, not personal responsibility, is the high priest of the age.

William Jennings Bryan said it well: “Destiny is no matter of chance. It is a matter of choice: It is not a thing to be waited for, it is a thing to be achieved.”

Our time is now. The era into which we are born is the era for which we have responsibility, the era for which we are meant to be blessing.

The fundamental problem of life, obviously, is not a lack of opportunity. It is a lack of soul, of what Confucians call “righteousness,” of what Buddhists call “awareness,” of what Jews call “tsedakah,” of what Christians call “contemplative consciousness.”

Too often, while we wait for life, it passes us by, leaves us up to our hearts in dissatisfaction and over our heads in wanting.

But life is every grain of sand in the hourglass. And it is running. And once run it is gone forever.

Joy is the energy to carry on through dull days knowing that miracles can happen in the future because we have seen them in the past.

There is a another kind of tension far beyond adversity, however. Adversity, at least, gets our attention. But joy we take for granted. Joy we consider birthright and wait to inherit in outrageous proportions.

No, those who live by measuring sticks rather than by the meaning of the present moment are intent on gaining and grasping. Letting go is not virtue to them. Letting go is loss.

To this mind, it is never possible to simply go on, past the things of the past to the realities of the present.

The tension lies in being able to let go of the past. In a society of strivers, however, enough is never enough. In an achievement-driven society, life is not a thing of seasons; life is a product to be perfected and preserved.

“No one is more unhappy than the one who is never in adversity; the greatest affliction of life is never to be afflicted at all.”

Because nothing is permanent, nothing is deadly.

The problem with life is that it flows and is not graspable. The beauty of life is that it runs and does not stop.

Each of us lives, then, striving for an invisible finish line, a sun-covered summit, a grail in life that, once we reach it, we are certain will bring with it not only present satisfaction but perpetual peace as well. We live wanting to get it right. We go on searching for the secret to having it all.

The insight holds a terrible truth. We are our own captors.

is not what we see happening on the outside. Life is what goes on inside in the quiet, murky waters of our souls.

Life with all its complexity made it very clear: life is not a drama made up of isolated scenes, each of which is meant to be resolved once and for all. Instead, I came eventually to realize, life is a series of experiences, all of them important, all of them here to be plumbed and squeezed and sucked dry, not for their own sake but so that we may come to know ourselves.
