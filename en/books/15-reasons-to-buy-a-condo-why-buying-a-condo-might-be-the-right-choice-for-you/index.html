15 Reasons to Buy a Condo; Why Buying a Condo Might Be the Right Choice for You

by Tim Sutter

My note

Choosing where to put your capital is just as much about time and freedom as it is about money, and this book’s take on condos really aligns with that. The idea of minimizing upkeep to maximize life — especially when most of our waking hours are spent elsewhere — feels like a practical step toward financial sovereignty and long-term thinking. It’s a reminder that sometimes less space and more convenience can be the smartest allocation of both resources and energy.

I suppose I lean to a more minimalist, convenient lifestyle, but I believe that in most cases when you are single or married without children, you should at least consider a condo as an option when buying a property.

When you get married or move in with somebody, it does make a little bit more sense to get away from a condo and into a house, if only to put more space between the two of you for when it is necessary.

Some people enjoy doing yard work, and that is good for them. I don't see it as a good use of my time. I would much rather be reading, writing, watching TV, lifting weights, playing poker, sleeping, or just about anything else before I would voluntarily pull weeds or cut grass.

Most of us work in cities. If there is a nice condo close to where you work and it is a decent location to live in, you might want to think about buying there. This was the single biggest reason for my decision to purchase a condo.

It blows me away what some people will put themselves through to get to work every single day, just so that they can own a detached house or live in a small town.

Commuting is terrible. In my mind, it is one of the worst things anybody can condemn themselves to.

If all you are doing is going to work and spending five or fewer waking hours at home each day, is it really necessary for you to have 2,000 square feet of space?

Even though most people would prefer to have a big house with excessive amounts of bedrooms and bathrooms, there are plenty of sensible people who see the value in a less consumer-driven lifestyle. This was certainly a motivating factor for me.

If you are willing to pay and value the time it takes to carry out these tasks enough to spend the money, then the convenience factor just might be one that pushes you over buying threshold for a condo.

Personally, I am happy to not do any kind of yard maintenance.

There is no right or wrong way to live; it is just a matter of choice. Some people want to relax when they get home from work and either read or watch TV, while others find solace in using a lawnmower.

What living in a condo lacks in control, it certainly does its best to make up for in convenience.

You can't really buy a house and be steps away from a subway line, movie theatre, art gallery, museum, major shopping district, and strip of night clubs.

A piece of land will be worth more money because it is in a more populated area, among other factors.
