The Sovereign Child

by Aaron Stupple

My note

The idea that there’s no fixed “true self” waiting to be found really resonates with how I think about growth and capital allocation — it’s all about testing, iterating, and adapting to better problems over time. This book’s take on creativity as a constant, internal process mirrors Bitcoin’s evolving narrative: sovereignty isn’t a destination, but an ongoing practice we build from within.

We don’t have a flawed human nature, an inner brute, in need of control. We instead have many competing ideas about what to do next, including desires for quick gratification as well as for long-term goals. The way to select the best choice is to compare the reasons for those choices, not to blindly and forcibly reject those we label as brutish and declare war on them.

Adherents to this model often spend a lot of time in school, waiting for the self to reach some end state that only then can apply itself to a suitable problem in the external world.

In contrast, life as a journey of self-discovery is a model of stasis. The journey maximizes experiences, travels the world, and tries out various life partners and living situations.

Fortunately, living life as a fun, dynamic process of engaging with better and better problems can begin today. There is no need to hold real life in abeyance until one’s true self is revealed. The real work begins now.

Popper’s epistemology shows why: a true, essential, and unchanging self would represent an ultimate truth, one that can’t be altered or improved upon. Let’s imagine you’ve found your true self. How would you know? How would you know that this true self won’t change?

The notion that a major project in life is to find one’s true self is a harmful idea. People don’t have a true self, essential and unchanging, and life is not a journey in search of it.

The main reason that some people are more creative than others is not that they were born that way. It’s that they have not learned, via shame, punishment, or simple conformity, to suppress it.

It is at the core of what makes humans unique, something we all use all the time as life throws problem after problem at us.

Therefore, creativity is central to all understanding. It’s not a sideshow or a pastime. It’s not just for people who indulge in the arts or eccentrics who tinker in their garage.

Knowledge is always created, never consumed.

The bucket theory of knowledge isn’t just partially wrong, it is completely wrong. All new knowledge comes from within and none from without.

By adult standards, children are astonishing learners. We would be in awe of an adult friend who learned in four years what a four-year-old has learned in their short lifetime.

In the same way, the creation of all human knowledge requires first conjectures, and only then do we check these conjectures against internal criticisms and the external world.

When genes “learn” to adapt to their environment, they must first mutate, and only then does the external environment select which among the genetic variants is best adapted to it.

knowledge never comes from the outside, just like the genetic changes of biological evolution are never acquired from the environment.

In a controlled setting like school, this means conjecture and guesswork are forbidden.

The conventional view is that knowledge is taught or acquired from the outside, that it is poured into the mind like water pouring into a bucket. Popper called this the bucket theory of knowledge.

One of Karl Popper’s famous maxims is that all life is problem-solving. Parenting is teaching children how to live, which means how to solve problems.

Kids’ ideas are just as valid as adults’, and they should be taken seriously and accounted for in any solution to any conflict.

Taking Children Seriously is simply the recognition that, in the realm of parenting, the source of knowledge does not determine its validity, that knowledge does not require authoritarian justification.

Mistakes are the smoke that signals the fire of progress. Learning is a process of correcting mistaken guesses at solutions until we find a guess that works.

knowledge and make progress, mistakes proliferate.

Justified knowledge marks the end of progress. However, in Popper’s model, knowledge is never justified, it is never validated as true, and therefore mistakes are treated as inevitable.

What’s worse, when knowledge has been deemed truly justified, that means it cannot be mistaken and therefore cannot be improved.

Mistakes are always embarrassing in the justificationist model, because mistakes mean that the source of knowledge is not reliable.

Critical rationalism says that explanations are never justified. Rather, candidate explanations are “simply” conjectured, and then we come up with reasons why any of them might be wrong.

But this just raises the same pesky question—who decides what counts as evidence?

Modern justificationists hold no truck with divine authority, but they’ve replaced the priests with “the” data, or “the” evidence, or “the” observations.

Knowledge Needs No Authority

Popper’s process has a common sense understanding: critical rationalism can be thought of as the idea that guessing and testing, trial and error, applies to all human endeavors.

The process of human knowledge growth may be most evident in science, where the conjectures are better known as hypotheses, the criticisms as experiments.

Selection consists of, first, criticizing all of our candidate guesses and, second, choosing only the guess that seems to work best.

the engine of variation is conjecture, or creative guesswork.

Human knowledge growth is likewise the story of solving an endless sequence of problems through a process of variation and selection.

The mechanism of the growth of human knowledge parallels that of genetic knowledge. In broad strokes, genes solve new problems via variation and natural selection.

My goal in this chapter is to show that parenting can be more formally described as stewarding the growth of knowledge in children.

Chapter 10 presents the philosophy of knowledge—the epistemology—that underlies the entire book. Readers who prefer to start with the foundations are encouraged to begin here.

Rules limit freedom, and hence understanding, and therefore impair the parenting project.

What is the best way to foster understanding? To provide freedom and security for a person’s creativity to discover how the world works.

Parenting is the process of supporting a child until they understand the world well enough that they can support themselves.

Instead of focusing on rules, Taking Children Seriously focuses on fostering understanding.

It has no interest in what the best rules should be, or how to enforce them, because it has no interest in rules.

How do parents enforce these lines, limits, and expectations? Should they use harsh words, positive reinforcement, time-outs, confiscations, bribes, or breathing techniques?
