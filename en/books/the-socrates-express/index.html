The Socrates Express

by Eric Weiner

My note

Curiosity feels like the most valuable capital to allocate, and *The Socrates Express* reminded me why asking better questions is the real edge in both life and Bitcoin. It’s a quiet call to live with intention—choosing duty over obligation—and to keep refining how I show up, day after day. Hope it sparks that same restless wondering in you.

35 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

The King of the Question. Socrates didn’t invent the question, but he altered the way we ask them and, in turn, the answers they yield.

“Our culture has generally tended to solve its problems without experiencing its questions.”

The Heart of Philosophy, by Jacob Needleman.

I wonder. Two simple words, yet they contain the seeds of all philosophy, and more. All great discoveries and personal breakthroughs began with those two words: I wonder.

Duty comes from inside, obligation from outside. When we act out of a sense of duty, we do so voluntarily to lift ourselves, and others, higher. When we act out of obligation, we do so to shield ourselves, and only ourselves, from repercussions.

This realization gets Marcus moving. He has a duty to get out of bed. “Duty” not “obligation.” There is a difference.

Perhaps the greatest obstacle is other people.

To read Meditations is to witness an act of philosophy in real time. Marcus is live-streaming his thoughts, uncensored. I am watching “someone in the process of training himself to be a human being,” as the classicist Pierre Hadot puts it.

Marcus, like all great philosophers, was a wisdom scavenger. What mattered was an idea’s value, not its source.

The difference between philosophy and talking about philosophy is the difference between drinking wine and talking about wine.

Marcus exhorts himself to stop thinking and act. Stop describing a good man. Be one.

What Marcus Aurelius fears most is not death but forgetting. He constantly reminds himself to live fully.

A compilation of reminders and pep talks. Roman refrigerator notes.

The Romans viewed Greek philosophy the way most of us view opera: something worthy and beautiful, and we really should go more often, but it’s so darned difficult to follow and, besides, who has time?

We’re all bodies at rest, waiting for an outside force to act upon us.

Not necessarily, says Hume. You can’t move from a statement of fact to a statement of ethics.

A moral “ought” never follows directly from a factual “is.” (That’s why the “is-ought problem” is also known as “Hume’s Guillotine,” since he cleaves “is” from “ought” and insists on a gap between the two.)

For some of us, though, mornings smell of simmering despair. If you don’t like your life, chances are you don’t like your mornings.

Likewise, great ideas don’t dusk on us. They dawn on us.

Ronald Reagan didn’t campaign on a slogan of “Late Afternoon in America.” It was his promise of “Morning in America”

Mornings provoke powerful, conflicting emotions. On the one hand, morning smells of hope. Every dawn is a rebirth.

Bad days follow bad mornings.

We are brothers, Marcus and I. He, running an empire and wrestling with his demons; and me, feeding the cat and wrestling with my demons. We have a common enemy: mornings.

Our demons do not haunt us at nighttime. They strike in the morning. We are at our most vulnerable when we wake, for that is when the memory of who we are, and how we got here, returns.

The relationship between train travel and sleep is, like most relationships, complicated.

When it comes to sleep, there are two types of people. The first type views slumber as a bothersome interruption of life, an inconvenience. The second considers sleep one of life’s unalloyed pleasures. I fall into the latter category. I have few ironclad rules, but one is this: do not mess with my sleep.

Too many of us hum along, cluttering our minds with the trivial and the silly, as if we have all the time in the world.

We always need wisdom, but we need different kinds of wisdom at different stages of our lives.

Schopenhauer talked to his poodle.

It was not the meaning of life that interested them but leading meaningful lives.

Wisdom is portable. It transcends space and time, and is never obsolete.

All fourteen are dead, but good philosophers never really die; they live on in the minds of others.

Philosophy, unlike science, is proscriptive. It not only describes the world as it is but as it could be, opening our eyes to possibility.

Will Durant’s 1926 book, The Story of Philosophy.

Miles Kington said: “Knowledge is knowing that a tomato is a fruit. Wisdom is not putting it in a fruit salad.” Knowledge knows. Wisdom sees.
