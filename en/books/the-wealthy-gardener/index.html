The Wealthy Gardener

by John Soforic

My note

Building wealth isn’t just about capital; it’s about how we spend the one resource we can never get back—time. Soforic’s focus on “impact hours” hits home, reminding me that intentional effort, day by day, shapes the freedom I seek through Bitcoin and smart allocation. This feels like a blueprint for turning philosophy into practical, long-term results.

I saw that most dreams died from lack of daily hours, And so I rearranged my schedule for more hours of work.

All dreams are paid for with our time. We build a foundation of success by using the days, one hour at a time.

We are always too busy to add to our schedules, but we either change what we’re doing, or we keep what we got.

AN IMPACT HOUR is sixty minutes of doing the right things that lead to a critical outcome. An impact hour is the opposite of a hollow hour, and it moves us forward toward our goals. It is an hour of effort that adds to the great volume of work that is required for achievement.

The days are long but the years are short,

Every condition we desire, including prosperity and wealth, requires a price to be paid—our outcomes reflect our hours. We miss the height of our fullest potential by not engaging the quiet parade of days.

Our potential is useless without our engagement of time.

At the very least, live with dignity and choose to contribute to a worthy cause, whatever you decide that is, by listening to your inner voice.

I was told that ambition for riches comes from the devil, But found that the prosperous life was a spiritual adventure.

Be wary of those who tell you to be content with your condition in life. Only you can know the condition that will satisfy your soul.

It is waking up without money worries. It is living without the pressure of time. It is the ability to spend one’s daily hours in meaningful pursuits. And it required me to grow spiritually in the struggle.

Prosperity is the power to take a walk in the woods on a weekday, pay for college tuitions, and live with choices, options, and power.

We never know the value of money until we know the fear of its absence. Money, like oxygen, is not too important until there is not enough of it. But when levels run low, every waking hour is consumed by it—either spent working for money or worrying about it.
