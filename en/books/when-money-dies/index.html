When Money Dies

by Adam Fergusson

My note

Watching how money’s value can unravel so quickly in *When Money Dies* makes me appreciate even more why true financial sovereignty matters—not just for preserving capital, but for preserving sanity and society itself. It’s a stark reminder that no system is immune to collapse, which is why thinking long-term and allocating capital with discipline feels less like a choice and more like a responsibility.

Just before the First World War in 1913, the German mark, the British shilling, the French franc, and the Italian lira were all worth about the same, and four or five of any were worth about a dollar. At the end of 1923, it would have been possible to exchange a shilling, a franc or a lira for up to 1,000,000,000,000 marks,

This is, I believe, a moral tale. It goes far to prove the revolutionary axiom that if you wish to destroy a nation you must first corrupt its currency. Thus must sound money be the first bastion of a society’s defence.

People’s trust in their currency is here a central theme. As it evaporates, they spend faster, the velocity of circulation increases, a little money does the work of much, prices take off, and more money is needed.
