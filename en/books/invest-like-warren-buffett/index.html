Invest Like Warren Buffett

by Matthew R. Kratter

My note

Finding businesses with durable moats and consistent pricing power feels a lot like seeking the same kind of sovereignty I value in Bitcoin—long-term strength with the freedom to act deliberately. This book’s focus on disciplined capital allocation and understanding what truly makes a company great aligns with how I think about preserving and growing real value over time. Thought you’d appreciate how Buffett’s principles echo our shared belief in thoughtful patience and clarity amid complexity.

Buffett typically invests in companies where the long-term debt could be paid off using 4 years or less of net profit.

A great business will show a consistent return on total capital greater than 15%.

The more money a company borrows, the riskier that company becomes.

There is one exception to this rule.  Sometimes a company will borrow lots of money (take on debt) in order to boost its ROE.

business with a higher ROE is a business that operates more efficiently, or is able to extract more profit from every sale.  If the ROE is consistently above 20%, it is very likely that what you are looking at is a great business with a moat.

Just as you would figure out your own net worth by adding up all of your assets (house, stocks, cash) and subtracting your debts (mortgage, credit cards), you can figure out a company’s “net worth” (or shareholder equity) by adding up its assets and subtracting its debts (also known as “liabilities”).

A great business will show a consistent return on equity (ROE) above 20%.

This is a direct result of its moat and its ability to raise prices consistently.

A great business will have earnings that show a smooth upward trend.

If you are a beginner, there is an easy way to get started.  I highly recommend the one-page stock analysis provided by Value Line.

Fourth, a great business will usually be one where the consumer needs to purchase the product or service again and again.

When you are researching a company, ask yourself if the company was selling the same product 10 years ago, and whether it will be selling the same product 10 years from now.

Third, a great business sells products or services that never go out of style, and require very little updating.

Great businesses occupy a significant slice of consumer mindshare.

Second, a great business usually has excellent brand recognition.  For example, even my six-year old can identify Apple, Starbucks, Disney, and American Girl (now a subsidiary of Mattel).

Before you invest in a company, you need to understand the products or services that it provides. It is even better if you personally use and love the company’s products or services.

First, a great business should be simple. Another way of saying this is that you are able to understand what the business does, and how it makes money.

look for simple businesses, with consistent performance, and favorable long-term prospects.

The company has little to no brand recognition, and no pricing power— any cost savings end up accruing to the customer, rather than to the owner of the business.

This is what a not-so-great business looks like. It produces a commodity product in a highly fragmented and competitive marketplace.

Time and money are too valuable to waste on researching or investing in a sub-par business.

Before we move on, it is important to remember that for Buffett, stocks are not just pieces of paper, or wiggles on a stock chart.  Rather, they are ownership interests in real-world businesses that entitle you to a share of the present and future profits of those businesses.

Buy shares in a few high-quality, well-run businesses at a fair price, and then hold them forever.
