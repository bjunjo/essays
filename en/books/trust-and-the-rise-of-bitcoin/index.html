Trust and the Rise of Bitcoin

by LQwD Fintech, Shone Anstey, and Stephen Thompson

My note

Seeing Bitcoin as more than just money but as a foundational upgrade to the internet’s plumbing really reshaped how I think about trust and sovereignty. The way Lightning channels enable trustless, instant transactions feels like the kind of infrastructure shift that finally gives individuals real control over their financial lives—something I keep circling back to in both capital allocation and long-term thinking. This book captures that subtle but massive shift, which feels essential as we navigate a future beyond traditional institutions.

Once you understand that Bitcoin is an extension of the internet and fundamentally alters its very plumbing, you will understand why it has tremendous value.

This technology breakthrough of a decentralized cryptographic timestamp service adds layer eight to the seven-layer OSI technology stack. It is a fundamental change to how the very plumbing of the internet functions by adding an open trust protocol layer.

They call this the Open Systems Interconnection model or OSI, a long name representing the technologies that make up the plumbing of the internet.

The internet, which grew out of ARPANET, is composed of seven technology layers.

Bitcoin is a globally distributed, cryptographic digital timestamp service enabling trust, and all use cases flow from this. Money is only the first step.

At its core, Bitcoin is a trust protocol layer of the internet.

This combination of decentralisation with real-time transparency is the core value proposition of the Bitcoin network: Trust.

The arrival of Bitcoin soon after could not have been timelier. It is an open trust network that relies on distributed computers and math to ensure integrity, where transactions are pseudo anonymous but can be viewed on the public network.

Bitcoin is the internet's trust layer, and it will change how trust is done in the digital age. Fixing the money is its first use case.

This book delves into the issues of trust and corruption, and why trust is essential for a functioning society.
