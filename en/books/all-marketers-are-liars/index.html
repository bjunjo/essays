All Marketers Are Liars

by Seth Godin

My note

Stories shape how we see the world and decide what’s worth trusting—something I think about every time I allocate capital or hold Bitcoin. Seth’s take on marketing reminds me that the narratives we believe aren’t just fluff; they’re the foundation of our financial sovereignty and long-term bets. It’s a good reminder that the stories we tell—and buy into—need to align deeply with who we are, or they fall flat.

My highlights

26 highlights from Kindle. These are the lines I stopped at.

Marketing is about spreading ideas, and spreading ideas is the single most important output of our civilization.

We (anyone with the ability to tell a story—online, in print or to the people in our communities) have the ability to change things more dramatically than ever before in history.

I believe marketing is the most powerful force available to people who want to make change. And with that power comes responsibility.

The best stories don’t teach people anything new. Instead, the best stories agree with what the audience already believes and makes the members of the audience feel smart and secure when reminded how right they were in the first place.

And most of all, great stories agree with our worldview.

Great stories don’t contradict themselves.

Runaway hits like the LiveStrong fund-raising bracelets take off because they match the worldview of a tiny audience—and then that tiny audience spreads the story.

If you need to water down your story to appeal to everyone, it will appeal to no one.

Great stories are rarely aimed at everyone.

Great stories don’t appeal to logic, but they often appeal to our senses.

First impressions are far more powerful than we give them credit for. Great stories don’t always need eight-page color brochures or a face-to-face meeting.

Great stories happen fast.

Talented marketers understand that the prospect is ultimately telling himself the lie, so allowing him (and the rest of the target audience) to draw his own conclusions is far more effective than just announcing the punch line.

Great stories are subtle.

As a result, no marketer succeeds in telling a story unless he has earned the credibility to tell that story.

Great stories are trusted.

People believe stories because they are compelling.

the path to profitable growth is in satisfying wants, not needs. (Of course, your product must really satisfy those wants, not just pretend to!)

Marketers profit because consumers buy what they want, not what they need. Needs are practical and objective, wants are irrational and subjective.

Marketers tell the stories, and consumers believe them. Some marketers do it well. Others are pretty bad at it.

Marketers are a special kind of liar. Marketers lie to consumers because consumers demand it.

Either you’re going to tell stories that spread, or you will become irrelevant.

This is a story about why marketers must forsake any attempt to communicate nothing but the facts, and must instead focus on what people believe and then work to tell them stories that add to their worldview.

All marketers are storytellers. Only the losers are liars.

“What’s your story?” “Will the people who need to hear this story believe it?” “Is it true?” Every day, we see mammoth technology brands fail because they neglected to ask and answer these questions.

We believe what we want to believe, and once we believe something, it becomes a self-fulfilling truth.
