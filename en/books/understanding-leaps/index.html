Understanding LEAPS

by Marc Allaire

My note

There’s something about using LEAPS that feels like carving out financial sovereignty in a world obsessed with leverage and margin calls. This book helped me see options not as risky gambles, but as tools for thoughtful, long-term capital allocation—kind of like how I think about holding Bitcoin. It’s a reminder that controlling downside risk while staying patient can be just as powerful as chasing big returns fast.

Technically, when a trader buys a LEAPS call and sells a short-term call option against this long position, the resulting position is a diagonal spread, not a covered write.

The trader who is trying to spruce up the returns of a covered write through the use of margin may, instead of buying the underlying stock with borrowed funds, choose to purchase a LEAPS call, against which short-term options can be written.

Using LEAPS as part of a “covered write” is simply an extension of that concept.

Against a long stock position (either shares already held or shares purchased for the purpose of establishing the position), call options are written.

Covered writing is probably the preferred conservative option strategy of numerous investors. For certain individuals, it is the only option strategy worth considering.

The returns on a long LEAPS position may not be as high as those generated by stock purchased on margin, but the possibility of margin calls has been eliminated, the downside risk limited, and the financing cost locked in.

3. For the investor who likes to purchase stocks on margin, LEAPS should be considered as an alternative.

For the investor who has the funds but is hesitant to purchase a given stock for any of many reasons, the “buy LEAPS and buy a T-bill” strategy may be appropriate.

If, as an investor, you do not have the funds to buy a stock you would like to hold, consider the “buy LEAPS and start to save” strategy.

The last point to address is that of holding period. Our example assumed that the LEAPS would be held for the full 2-year term. A more likely scenario would be for the position to be liquidated sometime before expiration when the underlying stock had reached its target price.

A second, and somewhat minor, advantage of the LEAPS over the margin purchase is that when one buys the LEAPS, one’s cost of financing is known and fixed.

The LEAPS buyer could see the price of BLND drop to $40 and not be forced to liquidate his position.

The worst-case scenario for the buyer of BLND on margin is to have the value of the stock drop shortly after it was purchased, sell the stock to meet the margin call, and then see the stock rally to new highs.

Purchasing LEAPS calls eliminates the possibility of margin calls, since the purchased options have to be fully paid for when the position is initiated.

The curse of the investor who purchases stocks on margin is the dreaded margin call.

Our starting point, therefore, is that the two strategies—buying stock on margin and buying the 40 call—will require approximately the same initial investment per share. The question that we now must turn to is how different are the outcomes from a return and from a risk perspective.

If purchasing a stock on margin is one way to leverage an equity position, so is buying calls.

LEAPS AS AN ALTERNATIVE TO PURCHASING STOCK ON MARGIN

An investor who sets out with the intention of exercising his long calls may, by the expiration date, decide not to do so. The fundamentals of the stock may have changed, or the stock may have risen as anticipated and may no longer represent a buying opportunity. Under this scenario, the investor could decide to simply sell his LEAPS calls, thus liquidating his position.

In other words, the lower the risk, the higher the amount of additional funds that will later be required.

The purchase of LEAPS calls will let this investor hedge his cash. Like the first investor, he must decide which strike price to purchase, as this will once again involve some trade-offs.

As underscored at the beginning of this chapter, there is a risk in not being in the market, that of missing out on a big move up.

But there is also the case of a second investor who does have the funds to purchase a given stock, but who is hesitant to jump into the market for one or a number of reasons.

Purchasing the LEAPS gives immediate exposure to the shares and gives our investor 2 years to save up the $80 necessary to exercise the option.

For the investor who currently does not have the cash to buy as many shares of FLL as she would like, it probably is worth it.

Since the goal is to own FLL shares, it is probably wise to commit as much capital as possible now, in order to end up with a lower effective purchase price later.

So the LEAPS buyer has to strike a compromise: To lower the eventual effective purchase price, more capital must be committed to the position when it is initiated.

Purchasing LEAPS calls may represent an effective way to hedge a cash position by providing participation in the market in general or exposure to individual equities with limited fund requirements and limited risk.

For others, risk means being out of the market: You are sitting in cash and the market goes up, leaving you not with a loss, but with a missed opportunity.

This chapter will look at buying calls from an investment perspective, that is, for those whose intention is to own the underlying stock at some point in time.

If the trader’s forecast is that this equity will be on the move for the next 2 years, then he would purchase the 2-year LEAPS.

The option’s expiration date should match the trader’s time frame: If the stock is expected to perform well during the next year, the trader would buy the corresponding LEAPS.

The choice between these two should be dictated by the trader’s expectations as to how quickly the underlying stock will rise.

Once the decision to purchase LEAPS and not shorter-dated options has been made, he need only decide between the 1-year LEAPS and the 2-year LEAPS.

Our preferred strategy is to allocate an equal dollar amount to each trade.

When someone purchases call options “for a trade,” our assumption is that this buyer has no intention of exercising the options and taking delivery of the underlying stock, although he or she will have the opportunity to change his or her mind later on.

For most traders, buying calls is the strategy through which they first get their feet wet in the options market.
